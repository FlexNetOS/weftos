## One-paragraph summary

This is the foundational article that established **soundscape ecology**
as a named discipline. The authors argue that the soundscape — the
complete sonic output of a landscape — is an ecological property
worthy of study in its own right, on par with the
structural, compositional, and functional measures that landscape
ecology already uses. They introduce the now-canonical trichotomy of
**biophony** (sounds produced by organisms), **geophony** (non-biological
environmental sounds like wind, rain, running water, thunder,
geomagnetic sources) and **anthrophony** (human-produced sound), and
ground it in three existing hypotheses: the morphological adaptation
hypothesis (MAH), the acoustic adaptation hypothesis (AAH), and
Krause's acoustic niche hypothesis (ANH). They propose a six-theme
research agenda (measurement, spatiotemporal dynamics, environmental
covariates, human impact on soundscapes, soundscape impact on humans,
soundscape impact on ecosystems) and demonstrate it with four case
studies — Krause's 40-year archive, the Tippecanoe "rhythms of nature"
study, Sequoia acoustic-niche work, and Tuscany dynamic soundscape
mapping. The paper does not propose a single numerical acoustic index;
instead it provides the conceptual scaffold into which Sueur-2008
(acoustic entropy H), Pieretti-2011 (ACI), Kasten-2012 (NDSI) and all
subsequent indices are slotted.

## Methodology

The paper is a **framework paper**, not an empirical study with a
single dataset. Its "methodology" is the articulation of a research
programme plus four illustrative case studies drawn from the authors'
own ongoing work.

**Case study 1 — Krause soundscape archive.** Bernie Krause's
40-year natural-sound archive is used as an illustrative library.
Examples cited: a 1996 Madagascar forest recording (1 min 28 s)
demonstrating acoustic niche separation; a Central African Republic bai
showing how landforms shape nighttime insect/frog/elephant soundscapes;
a Zimbabwe dawn chorus showing how landforms propagate baboon calls
(echo decay > 4 s) versus bird calls (~⅓ s).

**Case study 2 — Tippecanoe Rhythms of Nature.** Year-long monitoring
(Sept 2001 – Oct 2002, extended in later analyses) at **8 sites** in
Tippecanoe County, Indiana, spanning a land-use gradient from old-growth
forest to agricultural cornfield. Wildlife Acoustics Songmeters deployed
at each site. **>34,000 15-minute recordings** aggregated. The
spectrogram was discretized into **10 frequency bands** and for each
15-minute file the authors computed (a) a Shannon diversity index over
the 10-band energy distribution, (b) the Gini coefficient as an evenness
measure, and (c) the dominant frequency band. Acoustic activity was
used as a surrogate for sound-source abundance. Results were stratified
by land-use gradient (% urban/agricultural cover within 100 m), season,
and time of day.

**Case study 3 — Sequoia acoustic-niche hypothesis.** Four pristine
habitats — forest riparian, oak savanna, dry savanna chaparral, and
old-growth forest — monitored at dawn, midday, dusk, midnight (60-min
blocks). Randomly selected 11.5-second segments were analyzed to test
for spectral niche partitioning among animals (ANH) and for geophonic
masking effects.

**Case study 4 — Tuscany dynamic soundscape mapping.** Montane forests
in Tuscany, Italy. Spatial mapping of soundscape metrics over time.

**Quantitative acoustic framework.** The paper recommends that
soundscape measurements be recorded digitally (typical consumer
recorders at 44.1 kHz/16-bit PCM) and stored as uncompressed WAV, with
intensity reported in dB (or dBFS for device-level calibration).
Spectrograms are the primary visualization. The **Sender–Propagation–
Receiver (SPR) model** from classical bioacoustics frames individual
communication events: a sender encodes a message into physical
parameters (frequency, amplitude, directionality, point of origin), the
propagation medium shapes the signal, and the receiver decodes back
into information. For soundscapes, the model is extended to an
acoustic sensor **array** producing a time-varying acoustic **field**.

## Key results

Because this is a framework paper, the numbers are illustrative rather
than exhaustive.

- **Tippecanoe dawn/dusk peaks**: Spectral Shannon diversity at natural
  sites peaked at ~07:00 (dawn chorus) and ~22:00 (dusk). **Night-time
  diversity was twice the midday value** at all sites except the
  cornfield. Late-summer was the peak-entropy season (birds + cicadas).
- **Land-use gradient**: Acoustic activity, frequency-band diversity,
  and evenness all decreased monotonically with % urban/agricultural
  cover within 100 m.
- **Seasonal spectral shift**: In May all 8 Tippecanoe sites were
  dominated by low-frequency bands (bands 1–2); by August/September
  bands 3–8 became prominent at natural sites, driven by the insect
  chorus.
- **Sequoia ANH evidence**: In the old-growth Crescent Meadow site,
  animal vocalizations occupied the 200 Hz – 9 kHz range and filled
  **~82% of the spectrogram**, with organisms partitioning the
  spectrum by group (flies at 200 Hz, frogs 600 Hz – 2 kHz, birds up
  to 8.7 kHz).
- **Madagascar niche separation example**: ~30 vocalizing birds
  observed to occupy non-overlapping frequency/time niches; a
  sickle-billed vanga (*Falculea palliata*) emitted at ~1 kHz, far
  below the other species.
- **Source spectral classes**:
  - Urban/mechanical sound: concentrated below **4 kHz**.
  - Rain/wind: 100 Hz – 1 kHz (light), up to 8 kHz (heavy).
  - Insects: 3–4 kHz and 6–8 kHz (crickets, katydids, cicadas).
  - Amphibians: 2–5 kHz.
  - Most birds: 2–6 kHz (up to 10–12 kHz for some tropical species).
  - Baboons: echo decay > 4 s vs ~0.33 s for birds at the same site.
- **Anthrophony shifts animal signals**: documented examples include
  American robins singing at night in urban areas (Fuller 2007), song
  sparrows raising their minimum-note frequency in ambient-noise
  environments (Wood & Yezerinac 2006), great tits raising pitch in
  urban noise (Slabbekoorn & Peet 2003), and nightingales singing
  more loudly (Brumm 2004).

## Strengths

- **Single best-cited framework paper** in the field. Canonical
  source for the biophony/geophony/anthrophony trichotomy. Every
  subsequent soundscape study — terrestrial and marine — cites it.
- **Explicitly cross-disciplinary**, bridging landscape ecology,
  bioacoustics, animal communication theory, urban planning (Southworth
  1969), and conservation.
- **Includes audio supplementary material** — 34+ sound files attached
  to the online article, pioneering a practice that later marine
  soundscape papers (e.g., SurfPerch, Staaterman 2014) have adopted.
- **Actionable research agenda**: the six themes each correspond to a
  distinct experimental design and have driven the field's output for
  the 14 years since.
- **Methodologically pluralist**: recommends spectrogram visualization,
  Shannon index, Gini coefficient, and land-use covariates together
  rather than privileging any single metric.

## Limitations

- **No unified acoustic index is proposed.** The numerical backbone of
  soundscape ecology (ACI, NDSI, BI, H, ADI, AEI) was built by later
  papers. Pijanowski-2011 points at the need but leaves the formulas
  to Sueur-2008 (H), Pieretti-2011 (ACI), Kasten-2012 (NDSI).
- **Terrestrial bias.** The paper barely mentions aquatic/marine
  soundscapes; underwater propagation, refraction through sound-speed
  profiles, and anthropogenic noise from shipping/sonar/wind farms are
  treated as a footnote rather than a first-class topic. Marine
  soundscape ecology had to be re-scaffolded in Miksis-Olds 2013
  and the papers that followed.
- **Weak on sensor calibration and referencing.** dBFS is mentioned
  but there is no treatment of referencing to 1 µPa (underwater) or
  20 µPa (in air), of transducer calibration, or of the
  amplitude-vs-bit-depth trade-off that matters for quiet soundscapes.
- **No statistical framework** for soundscape comparison. The case
  studies report means and visual patterns; formal hypothesis tests
  (ANCOVA, wavelet analysis, mixed-effects models) come from later
  papers such as Staaterman-2014.
- **No discussion of long-term storage, duty cycling, or data-
  management** at a network scale. For decadal monitoring, this is a
  critical gap that HARP-class systems (Wiggins & Hildebrand 2007)
  and their successors fill.

## Portable details

### Biophony / Geophony / Anthrophony source bands (terrestrial)

| Source | Typical band | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Anthrophony (road/HVAC/motor) | < 4 kHz | Concentrated low-frequency; masks bird/amphibian calls. |
| Geophony (light rain/wind) | 100 Hz – 1 kHz | Quasi-stationary during weather events. |
| Geophony (heavy rain/wind) | 100 Hz – 8 kHz | Broadband; masks most biophony. |
| Biophony (insects) | 3–4 kHz, 6–8 kHz | Periodic bursts; temperature-dependent rate. |
| Biophony (amphibians) | 2–5 kHz | Seasonal chorus (breeding). |
| Biophony (most birds) | 2–6 kHz | Dawn and dusk peaks. |
| Biophony (tropical birds) | up to 10–12 kHz | Long tonal calls; adapted to wind/rain masking. |
| Biophony (large mammals) | 100 Hz – 2 kHz | Long-range, low-frequency for landform propagation. |

The **marine analog** (Staaterman 2014, this survey) reassigns:

- Geophony: < 100 Hz shipping-independent wind/waves; 100 Hz – 1 kHz
  surface waves and bubbles.
- Biophony (fish chorus): 50 Hz – 2 kHz.
- Biophony (snapping shrimp): 2 kHz – 20 kHz.
- Biophony (odontocetes): 1 kHz – 100+ kHz.
- Biophony (baleen whales): 10 Hz – 1 kHz.
- Anthrophony (shipping): 10 Hz – 1 kHz.
- Anthrophony (seismic air-gun): < 200 Hz, impulsive.
- Anthrophony (mid-frequency sonar): 1–10 kHz.

### Spectral-Shannon diversity over N bands

For a recording whose PSD has been binned into `N` frequency bands
with normalized per-band energy `p_i = E_i / Σ E_j`, the spectral
Shannon index is

```
H_band = -Σ_{i=1..N} p_i · log2(p_i)       bits, 0 ≤ H_band ≤ log2(N)
```

The Tippecanoe study used `N = 10` bands (approximately one octave
each from ~40 Hz to ~20 kHz); normalized by the logarithm of N gives
an evenness score identical to Sueur's Hf when computed on the
Welch-averaged spectrum.

### Gini coefficient for band evenness

For sorted per-band energies `E_{(1)} ≤ … ≤ E_{(N)}`,

```
G = (Σ_{i=1..N} (2i - N - 1) · E_{(i)}) / (N · Σ E_j)
```

`G = 0` when energy is perfectly even across bands, `G → 1` when a
single band dominates. Useful as a complement to Shannon H because it
emphasises the *tail* of the band distribution.

### Dominant frequency band

For each window, `argmax_i E_i`. In the Tippecanoe study this was
tracked over a year and showed the seasonal shift from low bands (spring
geophony-dominated) to high bands (late-summer insect-dominated).

### Six-theme research agenda (directly portable to marine PAM)

1. **Measurement & analytical challenges** → PAM sensor arrays,
   calibration, data volume, acoustic indices.
2. **Spatiotemporal dynamics** → diel, tidal, lunar, seasonal cycles.
3. **Soundscape / environmental covariates** → correlate indices with
   SST, salinity, wind, current, bathymetry, SSP.
4. **Human impact on soundscapes** → vessel noise, seismic surveys,
   wind farms, sonar.
5. **Soundscape impact on humans** → quality-of-life, tourism.
6. **Soundscape impact on ecosystems** → larval settlement
   (Staaterman 2014), predator-prey interactions, acoustic masking of
   communication.

### Acoustic Niche Hypothesis as a sensor-design prior

ANH predicts that species partition the spectrum to reduce overlap.
For a detector, this means **channelization** along biologically
meaningful bands (e.g., Staaterman's 25–2000 Hz fish band and
2–10 kHz snapping-shrimp band) is more informative than uniform
octave bands.

## Sonobuoy integration plan — long-term PAM mode

The Pijanowski framework directly underwrites a `sonobuoy-pam`
deployment profile that is distinct from the tactical-detection
`sonobuoy-tactical` profile already sketched in the round-1 synthesis.

**Deployment model.** A long-term PAM sonobuoy is a semi-permanent
node, not an expendable tactical drop. It sits in a cabled or
moored configuration for **months to years**, is duty-cycled for
battery/storage management, and is retrieved periodically rather than
tracked in real time. It parallels the HARP, SoundTrap ST600, EARS,
and MARU form factors.

**Index set to compute on-buoy.** Following Pijanowski's six themes:

| Index | Purpose | Compute interval |
|-------|---------|------------------|
| Spectral Shannon H_band (10–16 bands) | Biodiversity proxy | Per 1-min window |
| Gini coefficient G | Band evenness | Per 1-min window |
| Dominant band index | Spectral tracking | Per 1-min window |
| Sueur H (Hf·Ht) | Overall acoustic entropy | Per 1-min window |
| Pieretti ACI | Biophonic activity | Per 1-min window |
| Kasten NDSI | Biophony/anthrophony ratio | Per 1-min window |
| Broadband RMS SPL (dB re 1 µPa) | Noise load | Per 1-min window |
| Dawn-chorus / dusk peak amplitude | Daily phenology | Per day |
| Band-energy CSV dump | Raw feature for retraining | Per 1-min window |

All indices should run on the buoy DSP/MCU, producing a
**compact metadata stream** (~100 bytes per minute, ~140 KB/day per
buoy) transmitted via Iridium SBD or LoRaWAN between retrievals. The
raw WAV stays on the buoy's internal storage for post-recovery analysis.

**Three-level storage hierarchy (echoes Wiggins HARP).**

1. **Uncompressed WAV** on the buoy (200-kHz sample rate if odontocete
   coverage is needed; 20-kHz otherwise). Stored continuously or on a
   duty-cycle (e.g., 5 min on / 10 min off). Primary scientific
   product.
2. **Indices + long-duration spectrogram tiles** (128 s × 1024 Hz)
   streamed ashore daily via satellite. Used for real-time monitoring
   and for rapid triage on recovery.
3. **Event snippets** (~30 s) for any detection whose score >
   threshold on any on-buoy classifier (Perch, BEATs, species HNSW
   lookup). Also streamed ashore daily.

**Batch retrieval workflow.** At buoy retrieval, the data block is
unloaded, WAVs ingested into a central data-lake, and the full index
suite is recomputed at higher resolution than the on-buoy version
permits. Indices + metadata become the primary searchable product;
WAV stays as the source of truth for retraining.

**Compute on-buoy vs on-shore.**

- **On-buoy**: acoustic indices (all cheap), broadband SPL, event
  detection via frozen Perch/BEATs embedding + HNSW lookup (v1),
  DEMONet MoE classifier (v2). All must fit in < 1 W average power.
- **On-shore**: K-STEMIT dual-branch retraining, Helmholtz-PINN/FNO
  propagation modeling, multi-buoy fusion, cross-site ecological
  inference.

**ADR recommendation.** The long-term PAM mode is a **separate WeftOS
deployment profile** (`sonobuoy-pam`) from tactical detection. Its
constraints — power, duty cycle, storage, acoustic-index emphasis
— differ enough from the tactical profile (real-time bearing,
low-latency classification, sub-minute detection) that they should not
share a single systemd unit / runtime configuration. Propose
**ADR-063: Long-term PAM deployment profile for sonobuoy-pam** to
codify this split, with this paper + Sueur + Wiggins + Staaterman as
the reference bibliography.

## Follow-up references

1. **Sueur et al. 2008** *Rapid Acoustic Survey for Biodiversity
   Appraisal*, PLoS ONE 3(12):e4065, doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0004065.
   The canonical formal paper for the acoustic entropy index H and
   its decomposition into temporal (Ht) and spectral (Hf) components.
   Analysed separately in this survey.
2. **Pieretti, Farina, & Morri 2011** *A new methodology to infer the
   singing activity of an avian community: The Acoustic Complexity
   Index (ACI)*, Ecol. Indic. 11(3):868–873,
   doi:10.1016/j.ecolind.2010.11.005. Introduces ACI, the index most
   often paired with H for soundscape characterisation.
3. **Kasten et al. 2012** *The remote environmental assessment
   laboratory's acoustic library: An archive for studying soundscape
   ecology*, Ecol. Inf. 12:50–67,
   doi:10.1016/j.ecoinf.2012.08.001. Introduces the Normalized
   Difference Soundscape Index (NDSI) for biophony-vs-anthrophony.
4. **Miksis-Olds, Bradley, & Niu 2013** *Decadal trends in Indian
   Ocean ambient sound*, JASA 134(5):3464–3475,
   doi:10.1121/1.4821537. Extends Pijanowski's framework to the
   marine decadal scale.
5. **Farina 2014** *Soundscape Ecology: Principles, Patterns, Methods
   and Applications*, Springer. Book-length expansion of this paper by
